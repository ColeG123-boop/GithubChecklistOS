# Appendix A – Business Context

## Type 1 – Stakeholders
There are two identifiable groups of stakeholders that can be differentiated when developing a stakeholder map.

- **Internal Stakeholders (Primary)**
- **External Stakeholders (Secondary)**

---

## Type 1a – Internal Stakeholders (Primary)

Internal stakeholders are the users who directly interact with the solution on a regular basis. These individuals form the **primary usage group of the system**, as they actively use or manage the solution.

Examples include:

- Visitors (Non-Registered Users)
- Staff
- Owners
- Customers (Registered Users)
- Local Community
- Development Team
- Administration Team
- Maintenance Team
- Design Team

---

## Type 1b – External Stakeholders (Secondary)

External stakeholders do not directly interact with the solution but may still influence how it operates or develops. While they do not directly impact the system's functionality, they may influence policies, standards, or public perception.

Examples include:

- Government
- Media
- External Partners
- Hosting Company
- Auditors
- General Public

---

# Type 2 – Constraints

When developing a solution, it is essential to understand and address the following constraints:

- Time Required
- Total Budget
- Technology Needed
- Legal Considerations

---

## Type 2a – Time Required

Understanding the time required to complete a project helps create a **clear development timeline** and establishes expectations for progress. A timeline is an effective project management tool for both digital and physical projects.

Benefits include:

- Clear milestones
- Increased urgency
- Better project organisation
- Improved delivery within the set timeframe

---

## Type 2b – Total Budget

Setting a clear budget is essential as it establishes financial boundaries between management and the development team. It ensures fairness and helps keep the project financially sustainable.

A budget helps:

- Track financial progress
- Maintain profitability
- Control spending
- Align expectations between teams

---

## Type 2c – Technology Needed

Identifying required technology ensures the development team has access to the tools needed to meet the expected standards set by management.

This ensures that:

- The system performs as expected
- Developers can meet technical requirements
- The final solution operates efficiently

---

## Type 2d – Legal Considerations

Digital projects must follow legal guidelines to ensure that users and customer data are protected. Organisations must comply with legislation to avoid legal consequences.

Examples of relevant legislation include:

- Data Protection Act
- GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation)

---

# Type 3 – Empathy Mapping

An **empathy map** is a visual framework that helps designers and teams understand their users by exploring their perspectives, thoughts, feelings, and behaviours.

Empathy maps help teams:

- Understand user experiences
- Improve design decisions
- Predict user behaviour
- Develop better solutions

Empathy maps are divided into **four categories**:

### Says
User statements such as:
- Quotes
- Declarations
- Catchphrases

### Thinks
User thoughts and internal considerations including:
- Hopes
- Concerns
- Expectations

### Feels
User emotions and feelings when interacting with the solution.

### Does
User behaviours and actions while using the system.

---

# Type 4 – SWOT Analysis

A **SWOT analysis** identifies:

- Strengths
- Weaknesses
- Opportunities
- Threats

It is used to analyse projects or businesses and helps identify areas for improvement and opportunities for growth.

---

## Type 4a – Strengths

Strengths refer to aspects of the project that perform well.

Key considerations include:

- What is going well?
- What is unique about the project?
- How is it better than competitors?

Analysing strengths helps identify what already works effectively.

---

## Type 4b – Weaknesses

Weaknesses refer to the limitations or problems within the project.

Key considerations include:

- How do we rank against competitors?
- What can be improved?
- Are there complaints or recurring issues?

Identifying weaknesses early helps improve the overall quality of the solution.

---

## Type 4c – Opportunities

Opportunities are potential improvements or areas where the project can grow.

Key considerations include:

- What do competitors offer?
- Are there gaps within our services?
- How can weaknesses be improved?

Opportunities help guide future development and innovation.

---

## Type 4d – Threats

Threats are **external factors** that could negatively impact the project.

Examples include:

- Competitors outperforming the project
- New market trends
- Changes in industry regulations
- System outages or global events

Identifying threats allows organisations to prepare and reduce risks.

---

# Type 5 – User Personas / Stories

User personas are fictional representations of the target customer, based on real data and research.

They include information about:

- Goals
- Needs
- Behaviours
- Pain points

User personas help designers and developers better understand their audience.

### User Story Format

```
As (name), I want to... (goal or action)
```

Example:

```
As Jake, I want to begin using renewable energy and start using green technology.
```

# Appendix B – Functional and Non-Functional Requirements

## Type 1 – Functional Requirements

Functional requirements describe the **specific features and operations a system must perform** to meet business and user needs.

They define:

- What the system should do
- How it interacts with users
- How it interacts with other systems

Understanding system functionality helps clearly define these requirements.

---

## Type 2 – Non-Functional Requirements

Non-functional requirements describe **how the system performs**, rather than what it does.

They focus on system quality attributes such as performance, reliability, and user experience.

### Key Areas

- **Performance** – Speed and responsiveness
- **Security** – Protection against unauthorised access
- **Usability** – Ease of use
- **Reliability** – System stability and availability
- **Scalability** – Ability to handle growth
- **Maintainability** – Ease of updates and fixes
- **Portability** – Ability to run in different environments

These requirements ensure the system remains efficient, secure, and maintainable.

---

# Appendix C – Problem Decomposition

## Type 1 – Problem Decomposition

Problem decomposition involves breaking down a large problem into smaller, manageable tasks.

This helps:

- Simplify complex processes
- Identify dependencies
- Allow easier implementation

The tasks can then be followed by a system or a person with minimal prior knowledge.

### Example – Brushing Your Teeth

1. Get the toothpaste  
2. Get your toothbrush  
3. Wet your toothbrush  
4. Apply toothpaste  
5. Put toothpaste back  
6. Brush teeth in a circular pattern for 2 minutes  
7. Spit out the toothpaste  
8. Rinse your mouth  
9. Rinse your toothbrush  
10. Put your toothbrush back

# Appendix D – Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)

## Type 1 – Key Performance Indicators

A **Key Performance Indicator (KPI)** is a measurable value used to evaluate how effectively an organisation is achieving its objectives.

KPIs help organisations:

- Track progress toward goals
- Measure operational performance
- Compare performance against competitors

They are essential for long-term performance monitoring.

### Example KPI

**Site Response Time < 2 seconds**

This allows:

- Faster navigation
- Improved user experience
- Reduced waiting times
- Increased business efficiency

A faster response time can also provide a competitive advantage if other companies have slower systems.
